The Short Version

What this plan does

At a glance

  • A real path back to lifting. Three phases: (1) re-learn correct pelvic floor activation and full relaxation plus 360° breathing, (2) integrate the pelvic floor with the deep core through bridges, bird dogs, dead bugs, heel slides and clams, then (3) reintroduce load using an exhale-on-effort pressure strategy — light first, progressing slowly.
  • The non-negotiable rule. Never bear down or hold the breath against a closed throat under load while rebuilding. Stop or regress any exercise that brings on increased heaviness, bulging, dragging or new leakage during or after the session. Prolapse tolerance is highly individual.
  • The evidence is encouraging. A 6-month pelvic floor training program improved prolapse symptoms and produced measurable anatomical improvement in a blinded trial — and large survey data shows women who lift heavy do not report more prolapse symptoms. A careful return to lifting is a research-supported goal.

The research, in plain terms

In an assessor-blinded randomized trial of 109 women with stage I–III prolapse, six months of pelvic floor muscle training improved symptoms and helped lift the prolapse by one stage in roughly one in five women, versus far fewer in the untrained group.

Brækken, Majida, Ellström Engh & Bø — American Journal of Obstetrics & Gynecology, 2010.

In a survey of 3,934 women who lift for exercise, those lifting the heaviest loads did not report prolapse symptoms more often than those lifting light — the authors found no increased prevalence of prolapse symptoms among heavy lifters.

Forner, Beckman & Smith — International Urogynecology Journal, 2020. (Associational, self-reported — read as "not clearly harmful," not "protective.")

PHASE 01

Weeks 1+ · Start here

Symptom Relief & Foundation

Reconnect with the pelvic floor, learn to both contract and fully relax it, establish 360° breathing, fix posture, and stop the habits that push pressure downward. Spend at least 2–4 weeks here; it can run alongside everything that follows.

01

Diaphragmatic / 360° "Connection" Breath

Targets
Diaphragm–pelvic floor coordination; teaches relaxation and the pressure system that protects against prolapse.
How-to
Lie on your back, knees bent (or sit tall). One hand on the lower ribs, one on the belly. Inhale slowly through the nose into the lower ribs, belly and back so the ribcage expands in all directions — feel the pelvic floor gently relax and drop. Exhale slowly through the mouth, as if through a straw, and feel the pelvic floor and lower belly gently recoil upward.
Dosage
5–10 slow breaths, 2–3× per day. Use any time you notice tension or breath-holding.
Avoid
Belly-only "puffing" that pushes the abdomen forward; chest-only shallow breathing; forcing the breath.
Prolapse note
Keep the exhale gentle — never bear down. This is your relaxation tool; ease off if pressure increases.
02

The Kegel — done correctly (contract and fully relax)

Targets
Pelvic floor strength, endurance and coordination.
How-to
On an exhale, squeeze and lift the muscles around the back passage, vagina and urethra — as if stopping wind and the flow of urine, drawing up and in. Then fully release back to resting length on the inhale; the "letting go" matters as much as the squeeze. Keep buttocks, thighs and upper belly relaxed; keep breathing.
Dosage
Build toward holds of 6–8 sec with an equal-length full relaxation between — working up to 3 sets of 8–12 near-maximal contractions daily, in different positions with legs apart. Finish each set with 3–4 fast "quick-flicks" (sharp squeeze, instant release) for the cough/sneeze reflex. Begin lying down; progress to sitting, then standing.
Avoid
Holding the breath; squeezing glutes or inner thighs instead; bearing down rather than lifting up; never relaxing fully (over-gripping); doing so many that form fails.
Prolapse note
If heaviness or bulging increases, you may be bearing down rather than lifting, or doing too many — reduce volume. Don't routinely stop urine mid-stream (a one-off test only).
03

"The Knack" — pre-contraction before pressure

Targets
Reflexive pelvic floor support during coughs, sneezes and lifts.
How-to
Just before — and during — a cough, sneeze or lift, exhale and lift the pelvic floor; relax afterward.
Dosage
Use it functionally, all day, before any effort.
Prolapse note
This builds the exact habit you'll need for lifting. Keep it a lift, never a downward push.
04

Posture, Alignment & Toilet Habits

Targets
Reduces chronic downward pressure on the pelvic organs.
How-to
Stack ribs over pelvis — avoid tucking the tailbone or over-arching with a flared ribcage. "Come tall," as if gently lifting the organs. On the toilet, use a footstool (knees above hips), lean forward, breathe out, and never strain. Manage constipation with fluids and fibre.
Prolapse note
Straining on the toilet is one of the most common drivers of prolapse worsening — treat it as seriously as any exercise.
PHASE 02

Weeks 3–8+ · Overlaps Phase 1

Functional Strengthening

Connect the pelvic floor to the transverse abdominis, glutes and hips with controlled, low-pressure movement. The rule for every rep: exhale and gently lift the pelvic floor on the effort; inhale to return. Only progress an exercise once it's symptom-free during and after.

05

Glute Bridge with Deep-Core + Pelvic Floor Lift

Targets
Glutes, hamstrings, transverse abdominis, pelvic floor.
How-to
On your back, knees bent, feet flat hip-width. Exhale, gently lift the pelvic floor and draw the lower belly in, squeeze the glutes and lift the hips until shoulders–hips–knees align. Hold ~3–6 sec while breathing. Inhale to lower.
Dosage
8–12 reps, 1–3 sets; ~6-sec holds.
Avoid
Arching the low back / over-lifting; pushing the belly out; holding the breath; initiating with the back instead of the glutes.
Prolapse note
Exhale on the lift (the effort). A helpful cue: perform a Kegel as the hips rise. Stop if heaviness or a bulge appears.
06

Heel Slides

Targets
Transverse abdominis + pelvic floor coordination with limb movement.
How-to
On your back, knees bent. Exhale, gently engage the pelvic floor and lower belly, and slide one heel along the floor until the leg is straight — keeping the low back still and neutral. Inhale to slide back. Alternate legs.
Dosage
8–12 per leg; progress range as control improves.
Avoid
Low back popping or arching off the floor; chest flaring; breath-holding; losing core engagement as the leg extends.
Prolapse note
Only extend as far as you can keep the back stable and symptom-free.
07

Dead Bug

Targets
Deep-core anti-extension stability without spinal flexion — safer than crunches.
How-to
On your back, arms reaching to the ceiling, knees stacked over hips (tabletop). Exhale, brace gently, and lower the opposite arm and leg toward the floor while keeping the low back lightly pressed down. Inhale to return; alternate.
Dosage
5–10 per side, building slowly.
Avoid
Low back arching up; breath-holding; rushing the movement.
Prolapse note
Keep the range small enough to hold a neutral, stable spine with no downward pressure.
08

Bird Dog

Targets
Transverse abdominis, multifidus, glutes, spinal stabilizers; balance and coordination.
How-to
Tabletop position (hands under shoulders, knees under hips). Exhale and gently engage core and pelvic floor; extend the opposite arm and leg to body height, keeping the pelvis level and spine neutral. Inhale to return; alternate.
Dosage
5–10 per side, 1–2 sets.
Avoid
Rotating or dropping a hip; arching the low back; lifting the limb too high.
Prolapse note
Don't let the belly sag — that increases downward load. Keep it controlled.
09

Clamshells & Side-Lying Hip Work

Targets
Gluteus medius / outer hips — the "side walls" that support the pelvis.
How-to
Side-lying, knees bent ~90°, feet together, back neutral. Exhale, keep the feet together and lift the top knee a small amount without rolling the pelvis back. Lower with control.
Dosage
10–15 per side, 1–2 sets.
Avoid
Rolling the torso backward; using momentum; opening too far.
Prolapse note
Low-pressure and well tolerated — a reliable staple.
10

Modified Plank (wall → knees) — only when ready

Targets
Whole-core stabilization and shoulders.
How-to
Start as a wall plank (forearms on wall, body straight, feet back); progress to knees-down on the floor. Exhale, gently lift the pelvic floor and draw the belly in, hold a straight line head-to-knees, breathing throughout. Hold 10–15 sec, building gradually.
Dosage
3–5 holds of 10–15 sec.
Avoid
Sagging belly or low back; breath-holding; pushing pressure down.
Prolapse note
Skip full floor planks early. Regress to the wall at any sign of heaviness; progress only when symptom-free.
PHASE 03

Weeks 8–12+ · The lifting goal

Progression Back Toward Lifting

Reintroduce external load with a deliberate breathing and pressure strategy. This is where the bodybuilding goal lives — and it's realistic, provided progression is led by symptoms rather than a calendar.

The breathing strategy: "exhale on exertion" / "blow before you go"

How. Inhale during the easy phase (lowering into a squat); begin the exhale just before, and continue it through, the hard phase (standing up), gently lifting the pelvic floor as you exhale. Never hold the breath against a closed throat early in your return to lifting.

Why. Breath-holding and straining drive intra-abdominal pressure down onto the pelvic floor; a timed exhale lets pressure vent upward and recruits the deep core reflexively.

The nuance. Exhale-on-exertion is the right default for rebuilding. For very heavy near-max lifting, some lifters eventually use a controlled brace (Valsalva) — which is not the same as bearing down. That's an advanced step to try only once symptom-free at submaximal loads, ideally after a pelvic-floor PT assessment. Bearing down — pushing the pelvic floor down — is always wrong.

How to structure the return

  1. Start light, rebuild patterns. Bodyweight or light dumbbells, higher reps, perfect technique, exhale every rep. The Kenway leg and upper-body routines are the on-ramp.
  2. Master the movement before adding load. Box squats / sit-to-stand, hip hinges to a comfortable depth, supported rows and presses.
  3. Progress load gradually. Small increments (e.g. step up every few weeks) only when fully symptom-free during and after the prior level. Avoid big jumps.
  4. Modify the highest-pressure movements first. Be cautious with heavy overhead pressing, maximal deadlifts/back squats, breath-holding/Valsalva, and high-impact (jumping, running). A shallower squat or slightly lighter load often removes symptoms while keeping training effective.
  5. Consider support tools. Many women lift more comfortably with a fitted pessary (prescribed by a GP/gynaecologist) and/or supportive compression garments — useful bridges while strength builds.

Applies to every phase

Safety & Warning Signs

Signs you're doing too much — stop or regress the exercise

  • Increased heaviness, pressure, dragging, or a "bulge" / "stuck tampon" sensation in the vagina — during or after exercise, sometimes lingering a day or two.
  • A visible or palpable bulge that worsens or doesn't settle with rest.
  • New or increased urinary or bowel leakage with a specific exercise.
  • A period-like ache or low-back/pelvic ache that worsens with the workout (often eased by lying down with legs elevated).

Core rules

  • Never bear down or strain — not into exercise, not on the toilet.
  • Don't hold the breath under load while rebuilding; exhale on the effort.
  • Symptoms, not the calendar, guide progression. A symptom means back off that load or movement and rebuild.
  • Tolerance is individual — compare yourself only to your own symptom response.
  • Manage the basics: avoid constipation/straining, use a toilet footstool, keep loads close to the body, lift from a lunge or kneel for floor pickups, and avoid lifting from below the knee or above the shoulder where possible.

When to consult a doctor or seek further care

  • Symptoms worsen despite correct technique, or a bulge becomes constant or protrudes outside the vagina.
  • Inability to empty the bladder or bowel, severe pain, or heavy bleeding — urgent.
  • Before attempting heavy/maximal lifting or Valsalva — ideally get a one-off pelvic-floor PT assessment (in person or via telehealth) to confirm the muscles can support the loads she wants to train.

Put it together

The staged plan

  1. Weeks 1–4 · Foundation Daily 360° breathing (5–10 breaths ×2–3), the corrected Kegel protocol building toward 3×8–12 long holds + quick-flicks, "the Knack," posture and toilet habits, plus Dr. Bri's 3-minute hips-up decompression at day's end. Advance when: you can reliably contract and fully relax the pelvic floor and breathe without bearing down.
  2. Weeks 3–8+ · Functional strength (overlapping) Add the Phase 2 exercises (bridge, heel slide, dead bug, bird dog, clam, wall→knee plank), 2–4 sessions/week, exhaling on effort. Advance when: all are symptom-free during and after, with good form.
  3. Weeks 8–12+ · Return to lifting Begin light loaded patterns from Kenway's leg/upper-body routines; master the movement, then add weight in small increments every few weeks while symptom-free. Be cautious with heavy overhead, max deadlift/squat, Valsalva, and impact. A pessary or support garment can help. Note: guidance commonly suggests waiting ~12 weeks postpartum before intense lifting after an uncomplicated vaginal birth — let symptoms and your OB confirm timing.
  4. Throughout · Track it Keep a simple symptom log (heaviness/leakage 0–10, during and after each session). Any rise means regress that movement or load.
  5. Escalate when needed Return to medical care at the warning signs above; strongly consider at least one telehealth pelvic-floor PT visit before maximal lifting.

A few honest caveats

This guide is educational and is not a substitute for individualized assessment. Your wife has OB clearance for pelvic-floor strengthening; even so, a single telehealth pelvic-floor PT visit would meaningfully de-risk the return to heavy lifting.

The embedded videos are from credentialed physiotherapists (Michelle Kenway; Dr. Bri / Brianne Grogan, PT, DPT). If any player doesn't load — or doesn't match the title shown — use the "Open on YouTube" link beneath it, or search the title and creator; it's worth confirming each is the intended video before relying on it.

The lifting-and-prolapse survey evidence is associational and self-reported — read it as "heavy lifting is not clearly harmful," not as proof it's safe or protective for every individual. When in doubt, slow down and let symptoms lead.
