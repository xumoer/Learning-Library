Thoracic Spine Recovery · Tier 1

Foundation: calm, mobilize, wake up

The acute-phase tier — five low-load exercises to settle costochondritis, gently mobilize the mid-back, and re-activate a weak rhomboid. Pain-free only.

How to use this tier

The rules for Tier 1

  • This is the acute / foundation phase. Everything stays strictly pain-free — quality and feeling the right muscle matter more than load.
  • Do it daily. Short, gentle sessions; low load throughout.
  • Progress to Tier 2 only when symptom-free — these movements should feel easy with no chest or mid-back pain during or after.

The plan

Five exercises

01

Supported Thoracic Extension Breath

Targets
Thoracic extension mobility; rib expansion; gentle decompression of irritated costal joints.
Setup / How-to
Lie back over a foam roller (or rolled towel) placed across the mid-back around T6–T8, knees bent, hands cradling the head. Breathe wide into the ribs; on each exhale let the spine drape gently over the roller. Shift the roller a few cm up/down and repeat at neighboring segments.
Dosage
5–8 slow breaths per segment, 2–3 segments. Daily.
Avoid
Forcing extension; any sharp chest pain; cranking the neck.
Caution
Stay strictly pain-free — costochondritis flares with aggressive rib stretch.
02

Prone Scapular Setting ("Rhomboid Wake-Up")

Targets
Rhomboid and mid-trap activation / re-education.
Setup / How-to
Lie face down, arms in a "T" or by your sides. Without shrugging, gently draw the shoulder blades down and together — cue: "slide the blades into your back pockets." Hold and feel the muscle between spine and blade switch on, then fully relax. Equipment: bodyweight, or a light band for feedback.
Dosage
10 slow reps × 2, ~5-sec holds. Quality over load.
Avoid
Shrugging the upper traps; holding the breath; lifting with the arms.
Caution
If you can't feel the rhomboid, shrink the range and slow down — activation matters more than reps.
03

Quadruped Cat–Cow (segmental)

Targets
Gentle segmental thoracic flexion/extension mobility.
Setup / How-to
On hands and knees, slowly round the upper back toward the ceiling (cat), then sequentially extend (cow), leading from the mid-back rather than the low back. Move slowly, one segment at a time. Equipment: bodyweight, floor.
Dosage
8–10 slow cycles.
Avoid
Dumping into the low back; rushing; pushing into pain at end range.
Caution
Keep the T6–T7 region moving gently — small, controlled range while acute.
04

Tonal Seated Scapular Retraction (light, partial range)

Targets
Rhomboid / mid-trap re-education under light cable tension.
Setup / How-to
Set the Tonal arms low-to-mid, attach handles, sit or kneel facing the unit. With arms extended and elbows soft, simply squeeze the shoulder blades together a few inches — no full row, just the retraction. Use the lightest weight (~3–5 lb), Spotter/Chains off, slow tempo. Alt: a very light band held in front, same partial squeeze.
Dosage
12–15 reps × 2, light.
Avoid
Turning it into a big row; pulling with the biceps; shrugging.
Caution
This is activation, not strength — keep the load low while symptoms are acute.
05

Band Pull-Apart (high-rep, light)

Targets
Rhomboids, rear delts, postural endurance.
Setup / How-to
Hold a light band at shoulder height, arms straight. Pull the band apart by squeezing the shoulder blades, leading from the mid-back; return slowly. Keep the ribs down — don't flare them (protects the costochondral joints). Tonal alt: very light reverse fly.
Dosage
15–20 reps × 2, light band.
Avoid
Rib flare / arching; jerking; too-heavy a band.
Caution
Stop if it pinches the front of the chest.

Read before every session

Safety & Warning Signs

  • This is the acute / foundation tier — everything stays pain-free. Sharp or lingering chest or mid-back pain means stop.
  • Keep ribs "down," avoid rib flare and breath-holding — this protects the inflamed costochondral joints (costochondritis).
  • No aggressive end-range extension or rotation yet — the T6–T7 subluxation is acute, so keep mid-back motion small and controlled.
  • Prioritize feeling the rhomboid activate over adding load; quality first while the muscle re-learns to switch on.
  • Progress to Tier 2 only when these are easy and symptom-free during and after.
  • New loaded movement or worsening symptoms → check with your clinician.

A few honest caveats

This plan is educational and is not a substitute for in-person assessment by a clinician who can examine you directly.

The T6–T7 subluxation is acute. Before adding any new loaded movement — including the Tonal and band work here — clear it with your provider, and let symptoms, not a schedule, lead the progression.
